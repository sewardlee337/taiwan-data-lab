Project Overview

The Taiwan Institute for Strategy and Competitiveness (TISC) is conducting a series of studies on Taiwan's industry clusters to track their productivity and competitiveness. The methodology behind these studies is based on the Microeconomics of Competitiveness framework developed by Professor Michael Porter and his research team at Harvard Business School. We hope that, by identifying and nurturing clusters of productive firms and industries, Taiwan can secure and maintain its place as a competitive player in the global economy.

Clusters and Why They Matter

An industry cluster is a geographic concentration of related industries and institutions, including companies, suppliers, service providers, trade groups, universities, and government agencies. Economic research has demonstrated that clusters are hubs of economic productivity and technological innovation. For businesses, choosing to locate within an appropriate cluster may result in greater levels competitiveness. For governments, identifying and nurturing the right clusters offers the possibility of generating higher levels wage growth, job growth, business formation, and technological innovation.


Clusters can be broadly divided into two categories:

  • Traded clusters - Concentrations of related industries that sell goods and services to other regions and nations. The trans-regional nature of their business activities affords members of traded clusters more freedom in where to locate their facilities. Studies show a tight relationship between the existence of traded clusters and high levels of regional economic growth.

  • Local clusters - Concentrations of related industries that sell goods and services exclusively within their immediate geographic region. Employment in local clusters is generally proportional to the population within the respective region.

For a more information on industry clusters, see the "Clusters 101" page at the U.S. Cluster Mapping website.
